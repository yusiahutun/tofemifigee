Georgia police sergeant is demoted after leaving boy in freezing car

February 2024 · 5 minute read

Daniel Elzey (pictured) has been demoted to a police officer II and will be suspended for 30 days without pay

A police sergeant in Georgia has been demoted after his bodycam caught him leaving a 13-year-old boy in a freezing patrol car as a ploy to get him to talk.

Daniel Elzey has been demoted to a police officer II and will be suspended for 30 days without pay, Roswell Police Chief Rusty Grant confirmed to AJC.com.

Elzey was placed on paid administrative leave in July – more than six months after the incident occurred - and the department's Office of Professional Standards investigated.

He was seen in the video taunting the boy after he was stopped while driving a golf cart on Alpharetta Highway at around 1am on January 2.

After turning off the patrol car and rolling down the windows to allow the chilly winter air into the vehicle, Elzey is heard saying: 'Let him get a little chilly. Maybe that'll help.'  

The boy's clothing was already 'soaked and frozen' from being out on one of the coldest nights of the year, 11Alive reported.

An officer, C. Dickerson, had stopped and questioned the driver, who claimed to be 13 and said his mother had let him borrow the golf cart to go to Starbucks.

When Dickerson questioned the boy further, he was unable to provide the name of the apartment complex where he lived, his apartment number, or his mother's phone number.

His story about where the golf cart came from continued to change.

Suspecting that it was stolen, officers detained the teenager, handcuffing him and putting him in the back of Dickerson's car.

Over the course of 40 minutes, the boy continued to change his story – and he gave two different names.

He said his mother was out on a date and couldn't be reached. He said his mother was born in one year and then changed that date of birth.

He said he found the golf cart at an apartment complex, then behind Elkins Middle School.

Dickerson observed that he had women's jewelry in his backpack, and was dressed in all black with grass stains on his knees as if he had been crawling in the dirt.

Officers observed this male illegally driving a golf cart down Alpharetta Highway on January 2

'Getting cold yet?' Elzey was heard asking the teenager as he sat in the freezing patrol car

Still, the officers continued to question him. They advised him that if they could not release him into a parent's custody, they'd have to formally arrest him and take him to a juvenile detention facility for holding.

Then Elzey, a supervisor, arrived on the scene to assist with the questioning.

Elzey rolled down the windows of Dickerson's patrol car and left the teen handcuffed in the back seat. The temperature that night ranged from 12 to 18 degrees.

'He won't say anything if he's sitting there in the back seat and it's warm,' Elzey is heard explaining to Dickerson on her bodycam footage. 'So, he can sit over there and be cold.'

He adds: 'It's fine. Give him a few minutes to think about it. Let him get a little chilly. Maybe that'll help.'

The teenager is seen being handcuffed after he refused to give officers his mother's contact information

After 10 minutes, Elzey approaches the car and address the teen. 'Getting cold yet?' the sergeant asks.

'Yeah, I can see your breath, it's pretty cold back there,' he added as he continues to question the teen.

The boy finally gave police information about where he lived, and about half an hour later, officers released him into his mother's custody.

The mother expressed her frustration with the boy to the officers, according to Dickerson's incident report.

'She stated that she has two older boys that are both in college, and that [the juvenile suspect] has caused trouble several times in the past,' the report said.

'He is constantly in trouble and has been suspended several times.'

The report does not mention putting the boy in the freezing car with the windows down.

The golf cart had no serial number or identifying marks, and officers never figured out where it came from, and had it impounded.

Two Roswell Police officers were fired over an embarrassing bodycam video in July

The incident was revealed days after two other Roswell Police Department officers were fired over an embarrassing bodycam video in July.

Officers Courtney Brown and Kristee Wilson were filmed giggling as they were arresting motorist Sarah Webb after she was stopped for speeding past a police cruiser in April.

Suspecting the driver was intoxicated, the officers used a coin-flip app to decide whether to arrest her.

The officers agreed if the virtual coin landed on heads, she would be arrested, and if it landed on tails, she would be released.

The coin landed on tails. But perhaps forgetting, or not caring, Wilson called for Sarah's arrest, using the code '23'.

Laughing, Brown got out of the car to take Sarah into custody.

The incidents prompted Roswell city officials to undertake a department-wide review of the police force.

'I have authorized the issuance of a national call for proposals from qualified, independent firms to come to Roswell and scrutinize every aspect of our police operations, identify the issues, and make hard recommendations on solutions through a formal final report,' City Manager Gary Palmer said in a Facebook post on the city's page at the time.

'As your city administrator, I am stunned by the lack of professionalism and compassion displayed by some of our officers,' Palmer added.
